\subsection{Echo-aware audio signal processing for audio scene analysis}

When listening to a podcast, we can ask the following questions: \textit{Where are the speakers located? Are they in a room? How big is the room? Also, what is the shape of the room?}

\paragraph{Context:} Audio scene analysis involves extracting valuable information from microphone recordings, such as separating and locating sound sources. When sound travels in an indoor space, it interacts with the environment by being delayed, attenuated, reflected, and absorbed by obstacles. The resulting propagation retains the spatial information of the audio scene.
Most methods neglect or inaccurately estimate the \textit{entire} sound propagation, leading to erroneous results. This work, instead, emphasizes \textit{acoustic echoes}, linking sound sources' semantics to spatial attributes.

\paragraph{Contributions:} This work was conducted during my doctorate in the PANAMA team at INRIA Rennes. I proposed one deep learning (DL)-based regression method for echoes' time of arrival estimation exploiting the framework of \textit{virtually supervised} learning~\cite{di2019mirage}. Later, collaborating with C. \textsc{Elvira} (INRIA Rennes), we tackled this problem with \textit{continuous dictionary} learning~\cite{di2020blaster}.
In parallel, we extended source localization and source separation algorithms to their ``echo-aware'' form in~\cite{di2019mirage} and \cite{scheibler2018separake}, respectively. The latter was a collaboration with R. \textsc{Scheibler}\rmAff{ (Deep Mind, Japan)} and I. \textsc{Dockmani\'c}\rmAff{ (Univ. of Basel, Swiss)}. The key contribution showed that treating acoustic reflections as additional microphones improves spatial selectivity. Finally, we created a new database called dEchorate~\cite{di2021dechorate}, a resource for validating echo-aware methods. Such delicate data collection and software development (for annotating sound reflections) were conducted at Bar-Ilan University, Israel, with Prof. S. \textsc{Gannot}, a leading expert in the field. This database was later used by C. \textsc{Foy} to validate a deep-learning method for acoustic parameter estimation\rmAff{ (CEREMA Strasbourg)}~\cite{foy2021mean}.

3.2 Physics-informed neural networks for turbulence flow
When reading comics, we can imagine the continuous turbulent motion of a river from a few lines.
Similarly, can computers imagine continuous turbulent flows from a few sketches?
Context: This research tackles the problem of resolving high-resolution velocity fields from a few
off-grid observations, a crucial task in fluid dynamics, computer vision, remote sensing, and medical
imaging. The basic assumption is that partial differential equations (PDEs) can describe the
observed phenomenon, such as the Navier-Stokes equation for a turbulent flow. Unlike traditional
methods that rely on intensive numerical solvers or purely data-driven models, we focused on the
recently proposed Physics-informed Neural Networks (PINNs)2, trained on an objective featuring
a data-fit term and the PDE residuals computed via automatic differentiation. These models
show promising generalization capability even with a limited amount of training data. Yet, their
application was limited to â€œsimpleâ€ (i.e., low dimensional, low-order PDEs) problems.
Contribution: This work was carried out during my postdoctorate at LETG, UMR 6554, Rennes,
in collaboration with D. Heitz (CEREMA Rennes). I extended the unsupervised DL framework
based on PINNs with the following contributions: first, we engineered the neural backbone of the
model with the positional encoding used in NeRF models for novel-view synthesis in computer
vision3; second, to bypass the computational cost of high-order PDEs, we proposed a differentiable
â€œsurrogateâ€ model based on Kolmogorovâ€™s statistical theory of turbulence; finally, we proposed a
transformation of the output to ensure some PDE constraints by construction.
Originality: While most physics-inspired DL models rely on required discrete data representa-
tions (e.g., CNN-based architectures), our work uses meshless data, yielding differentiable functions
continuously defined over space. As part of the early development of PINNs, this project addresses
computational complexities in evaluating PDEs by introducing a low-complexity statistical model-
based regularizer coupled with a sampling strategy to evaluate the modelâ€™s residual.

3.3 Towards Neural Acoustic Fields
Imagine you are a music enthusiast who has always dreamed of attending a legendary concert.
Recent computer vision advances address this problem, offering immersive visual experiences. How
about the sound?
Context: This research aims to define physically coherent and user-controllable representations
of the acoustic field for mixed-reality (MR) applications. Classically, immersive sound environ-
ments are achieved by interpolating a discrete set of measured acoustic impulse responses (AIRs)
encoding the sound propagation between sources and sensors. However, most existing methods
are detrimentally affected by the scarcity of data. This work proposes an alternative approach
by constructing a continuous and differentiable neural acoustic field that captures the underlying
acoustics of a scene.
Contribution The target acoustic field must adhere to the wave equation PDE. Thus, the model
can leverage the PINNsâ€™ framework to overcome data scarcity and improve generalization. As the
first development, we applied this methodology to super-resolve the sound field around a userâ€™s
head as recorded by smart glasses (a concept connected to the Head-Related Transfer Function
(HRTF)) [dicarlo2024neural]. This research aligns with my teamâ€™s mission at RIKEN AIP to
develop innovative audio solutions for MR applications. In parallel, we addressed the problem
of AIR estimation in a multi-modal fashion, integrating the room structure registered by depth
sensors [kelley2024rir].
Originality: Spatial upsampling of measured AIRs is a longstanding challenge in audio process-
ing. The proposed approach, free from sampling grid dependencies, employs stochastic gradient
descent and is regularized by the wave equation, offering an attractive alternative. The main chal-
lenge here is to train the neural model correctly and define convergence in terms of data fitting or
physics, an issue that will be at the core of my proposed research program later.
Follow up: This project received the â€œKAKENHI - Grant-in-aidâ€ from the JSPS society. Cur-
rent research directions include applying these techniques to audio-related inverse problems, such
as speech bandwidth extensions with A. A. Nugraha (RIKEN AIP) and sound source localization
with Y. Bando AIST, Japan and M. Fontaine (Paris, France).
